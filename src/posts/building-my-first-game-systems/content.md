# Building My First Game Systems

Game development made programming feel physical.

A button press was not just an event. It was a jump, a throw, a camera shift, a sound, a little burst of feedback that told the player, yes, the world heard you.

That was the first thing I loved about game systems. They turn invisible logic into felt experience.

When I started prototyping, I wanted to build entire worlds immediately. Big environments, complex characters, inventories, quests, particles, enemies, menus, everything. The problem was that every big idea secretly contained twenty smaller systems I did not understand yet.

So I started smaller.

Can I move a player cube? Can I make it jump? Can I make the jump feel heavier or lighter? Can I make an object react when touched? Can I make a door open only when a condition is true?

Each small prototype became a lesson in cause and effect.

The beautiful thing about game development is that even tiny systems can feel alive when they respond clearly. A pickup that glows, a sound that lands at the right time, a camera that follows gently, a cooldown that teaches the player rhythm — these are not just technical details. They are part of the conversation between the game and the person playing it.

I am learning that game dev is not only about making things work.

It is about making things feel understandable.
